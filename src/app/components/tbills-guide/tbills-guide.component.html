What Are Treasury Bills?

T-bills are short-term debt issued by the U.S. government. They are one of the most widely held “cash equivalent” investments in the world — and the same instrument big banks use to earn yield on your deposits while paying you almost nothing.

What they are

A Treasury bill (T-bill) is a marketable U.S. government security with a maturity of one year or less. The Treasury auctions them regularly in standard terms — 4, 8, 13, 17, 26, and 52 weeks — and they are sold at a discount to face value (par).

You do not receive periodic coupon payments. Instead, the interest is built into the difference between what you pay and the $1,000 face value you receive at maturity. For example, if you pay $980 for a bill that matures at $1,000 in 13 weeks, your return is that $20 spread annualized over the holding period.

Issuer U.S. Department of the Treasury
Typical terms 4 – 52 weeks
Minimum denomination $100 (par), often bought in $1,000 increments
Interest form Discount — paid at maturity

How banks use your savings — and keep the spread

When you deposit cash at a major bank, it does not sit idle in a vault. Banks are in the business of maturity transformation: they take short-term deposits and invest them in higher-yielding, safe assets — very often U.S. Treasury securities including T-bills.

1 You deposit $50,000 Bank credits your savings account at ~0.01% APY.
→
2 Bank buys T-bills Same cash is invested in government paper yielding 4%+.
→
3 Bank keeps the spread You earn ~$5/year; the bank captures most of the Treasury yield.

This is not a conspiracy — it is the standard banking model. Deposits are a low-cost funding source; Treasuries are liquid, high-quality collateral and investment. The gap between what banks earn on safe assets and what they pay depositors is a core source of net interest income.

Full faith and credit of the United States

T-bills are backed by the full faith and credit of the U.S. government — the commitment that the Treasury will pay principal and interest using the federal government’s taxing power and borrowing authority.

  • Direct sovereign obligation. You are a creditor of the United States, not a customer of a private company. Repayment does not depend on a bank’s profitability.
  • Constitutional backdrop. The 14th Amendment prohibits questioning the validity of the public debt of the United States authorized by law — a legal foundation for honoring Treasury obligations.
  • Market history. Marketable Treasury securities have not experienced a missed principal payment. Debate over debt-ceiling politics is about timely payment mechanics, not the underlying obligation structure.
  • Not the same as FDIC. FDIC insurance protects bank deposits if the bank fails. T-bills are not “insured” — they are direct claims on the Treasury. Investors weigh sovereign credit risk instead of bank credit risk.

“Risk-free” is a market shorthand, not a guarantee of future outcomes. All investments carry some form of risk, including reinvestment, inflation, and policy risk.

Pros and cons

T-bills are a cash-management tool, not a wealth-building engine. Whether they fit depends on your time horizon, tax situation, and how much effort you want to spend vs. a savings account.

How to buy T-bills

TreasuryDirect

The U.S. Treasury’s official portal. You buy at auction or in the secondary market through your account. No broker commission, but you manage the account yourself.

Brokerage account

Fidelity, Schwab, Vanguard, and others let you buy T-bills on the secondary market or at auction. Often the easiest path if you already invest through a broker.

Money-market funds & ETFs

Funds like government money-market mutual funds or short Treasury ETFs hold T-bills and similar paper. Convenient, but you pay fund expenses and do not own the bills directly.

T-bill laddering

A ladder staggers maturities — e.g. 4-week, 13-week, 26-week, and 52-week bills — so something matures regularly. When one rung matures, you can reinvest at prevailing rates or use the cash. This balances yield with periodic liquidity without guessing a single perfect maturity date.

Quick glossary

Par (face value)
The amount a T-bill pays at maturity — typically $1,000 per bill.
Discount yield
Quoted yield based on the discount from par. Common in headlines and auctions.
Investment yield
Return based on amount actually invested — usually slightly higher than discount yield.
CUSIP
A nine-character identifier for a specific Treasury security issue.
